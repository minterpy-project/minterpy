Introduction#

Multivariate polynomial interpolation#

Polynomial interpolation goes back to Newton, Lagrange, and others[1], and its fundamental importance for mathematics and computing is undisputed. Interpolation is based on the fact that, in 1D, one and only one polynomial \(Q_{f,A}\) of degree \(n \in \mathbb{N}\) can interpolate a function \(f : \mathbb{R} \longrightarrow \mathbb{R}\) on \(n+1\) distinct unisolvent interpolation nodes \(P_A = \{p_i\}_{i \in A} \subseteq \mathbb{R}\), \(A=\{0, \ldots, n\}\), i.e.,

\[Q_{f,A}(p_i) = f(p_i)\,, \quad \text{for all} \quad p_i \in P_A \,, i \in A\,.\]

This makes interpolation fundamentally different from approximation, see Figure 1.

The famous Weierstrass Approximation Theorem[2] states that any continuous function \(f : \Omega\longrightarrow \mathbb{R}\) defined on compact domain, e.g. \(\Omega = [-1,1]^m\), can be uniformly approximated by polynomials[3]. However, the Weierstrass Approximation Theorem does not require the polynomials to coincide with \(f\) at all, i.e., it is possible that there is a sequence of multivariate polynomials \(Q_{n}^*\), \(n \in \mathbb{N}\) with \(Q_{n}^*(x) \not = f(x)\) for all \(x \in \Omega\), but still

\[Q_{n}^* \xrightarrow[n \rightarrow \infty]{} f \quad \text{uniformly on} \quad \Omega\,.\]

There are several constructive proofs of the Weierstrass approximation theorem, including the prominent version given by Serge Bernstein[4]. Although the resulting Bernstein approximation scheme is universal and has been proven to reach the optimal (inverse-linear) approximation rate for the absolute value function \(f(x) = |x|\) [5], it achieves only slow convergence rates for analytic functions, resulting in a high computational cost in practice.

There has therefore been much research into extending 1D Newton or Lagrange interpolation schemes to multi–dimensions (mD) by maintaining their computational power. Any approach that addresses this problem has to avoid Runge’s phenomenon[6] (overfitting) by guaranteeing uniform approximation of the interpolation target \(f : \Omega \longrightarrow \mathbb{R}\) and resist the curse of dimensionality, i.e., reach highly accurate polynomial approximations of general multivariate functions with a sub-exponentially depending demand of data samples \(f(p)\,,p \in P_A\), \(|P_A|\in o(n^m)\).

Lifting the curse of dimensionality#

To address the problem we consider the \(l_p\)-norm \(\|\alpha\|_p = (\alpha_1^p + \cdots +\alpha_m^p)^{1/p}\), \(\alpha = (\alpha_1,\dots,\alpha_m) \in\mathbb{N}^m\), \(m \in \mathbb{N}\) and the lexicographical ordered Multi index sets.

(1)#\[A_{m,n,p} = \left\{\alpha \in \mathbb{N}^m : \|\alpha\|_p \leq n \right\}\,, \quad m,n \in \mathbb{N}\,, p \geq 1\,.\]

This notion generalises the 1D notion of polynomial degree to multi-dimensional \(l_p\)-degree, i.e, we consider the polynomial spaces spanned by all monomials of bounded \(l_p\)-degree

\[\Pi_A = \left< x^\alpha = x^{\alpha_1}\cdots x^{\alpha_m} : \alpha \in A \right>\,, A =A_{m,n,p}\,.\]

Given \(A=A_{m,n,p}\) we ask for:

\(i)\) Unisolvent interpolation nodes \(P_A\) that uniquely determine the interpolant \(Q_{f,A} \in \Pi_A\) by satisfying \(Q_{f,A}(p_{\alpha}) = f(p_{\alpha})\), \(\forall p_{\alpha} \in P_A\), \(\alpha \in A\).

\(ii)\) An interpolation scheme Multivariate divided difference scheme (DDS) that computes the uniquely determined interpolant \(Q_{f,A} \in \Pi_A\) efficiently and numerically accurate (with machine precision).

\(iii)\) The unisolvent nodes \(P_A\) that scale sub-exponentially with the space dimension \(m \in \mathbb{N}\), \(|P_A| \in o(n^m)\) and guarantee uniform approximation of even strongly varying functions (avoiding over fitting) as the Runge function \(f_R(x) = 1/(1+\|x\|^2)\) by fast (ideally exponential) approximation rates.

In fact, the results of[7] suggest that the therein presented multivariate DDS resolves issues \(i) - iii)\) for so called Trefethen functions when choosing Euclidian \(l_2\)-degree and Leja ordered Chebyshev-Lobatto unisolvent interpolation nodes \(P_A\). Thereby,

\[|P_A| \approx \frac{(n+1)^m }{\sqrt{\pi m}} (\frac{\pi \mathrm{e}}{2m})^{m/2} \in o(n^m)\,, \quad A=A_{m,n,2}\,,\]

scales sub-expomemtially with space dimension \(m\) and

\[Q_{f,A_{m,n,2}} \xrightarrow[n\rightarrow \infty]{} f\]

converges uniformly and fast (exponentially) on \(\Omega = [-1,1]^m\). Figure 2 shows the approximation rates of the classic Runge function[6] in dimension \(m=4\), which is known to cause Runge’s phenomenon (over-fitting) when interpolated naïvely. There is an optimal (upper bound) approximation rate

\[\|Q_{f,A} - f\| \in \mathcal{O}_{\varepsilon}(\rho^{-n})\]

known[8],which we call the Trefethen rate.

In fact, the Multivariate divided difference scheme (DDS) scheme numerically reaches the optimal Trefethen rate. In contrast, spline-type interpolation is based on works of Carl de Boor et al.[9][10][11][12] and limited to reach only polynomial approximation rates[13]. Similarly, interpolation by rational functions as in Floater-Hormann interpolation[14][15] and tensorial Chebyshev interpolation, relying on \(l_{\infty}\)-degree[16], miss optimality.

Combining sub-exponential node (data) counts with exponential approximation rates, the DDS algorithm may lift the curse of dimensionality for interpolation problems of regular (Trefethen) functions[7].

The notion of unisolvence#

The pioneering works of[17][18][19] gave constructions of nodes \(P_A\) that turn out to be unisolvent for downward closed multi-index sets \(A= A_{m,n,1}\) or \(A =A_{m,n,\infty}\) given by \(l_1\)- or \(l_\infty\)-degree.

For multi-index sets \(A_{m,n,p}\), \(1\leq p \leq \infty\) and interpolation nodes \(P_A=\{p_{\alpha}\}_{\alpha\in A} \subseteq \Omega =[-1,1]^m\) a set \(\{q_{\alpha}\}_{\alpha \in A}\) of multivariate polynomials (e.g., \(q_{\alpha}(x) = x^\alpha:=x_1^{\alpha_1}\cdots x_m^{\alpha_m}\)), generating the polynomial space \(\Pi_A = \left<q_{\alpha} : \alpha \in A \right>\) the multivariate Vandermonde matrix is given by

\[V(P_A) = \big (q_{\beta}(p_{\alpha})\big)_{\alpha,\beta \in A} \in \mathbb{R}^{|A|\times|A|}\,.\]

For \(q_{\alpha}(x) = x^\alpha\), this results in the classic \(V(P_A) = \big (p_{\alpha}^\beta\big)_{\alpha,\beta \in A}\)[20]. If \(V(P_A)\) is (numerically) invertible, then one can interpolate f by solving the linear system of equations

\[V(P_A)C =F \,, \quad C= (c_{\alpha})_{\alpha \in A} \,, \,\, \quad F= (f(p_{\alpha}))_{\alpha \in A} \in \mathbb{R}^{|A|}.\]

This requires \(\mathcal{O}(|A|^r)\) operations with \(r>2\)[21][22], whereas the present DDS achieves quadratic runtime \(\mathcal{O}(|A|^2)\). Indeed,

\[Q_{f,A}(x)=\sum_{\alpha \in A}c_\alpha q_\alpha \,\, \in \Pi_A\]

yields the unique interpolant of \(f\) in \(P_A\), i.e., \(Q_{f,A}(p)=f(p)\) for all \(p \in P_A\). We therefore call a set of nodes \(P_A\) unisolvent with respect to \(\Pi_A\) if and only if \(V(P_A)\) is invertible, i.e., if and only if its null space \(\ker V(P_A) =0\) is trivial. The condition \(\ker V(P_A) =0\) is equivalent to requiring that there exists no hypersurface \(H = Q^{-1}(0)\) generated by a polynomial \(0\not =Q \in \Pi_A\) with \(P_A \subseteq H\). Indeed, the coefficients \(C\) of such a polynomial would be a non-trivial solution of \(V(P_A)C=0\).

However, even if \(P_A\) is unisolvent, the matrix \(V(P_A)\) rapidly becomes numerically ill-conditioned for higher dimensions or degrees when using the canonical basis \(q_{\alpha}(x) =x^\alpha\), \(\alpha \in A\). While previous approaches addressed this problem by tensorial interpolation[23][24][25], minterpy is based on the Multivariate divided difference scheme (DDS) that even for non-tensorial interpolation nodes such as the by default choosen Leja ordered Chebyshev-Lobatto nodes realises efficient and accurate polynomial interpolation.
